# Design Review: Fun, Consequence, & Agent Feedback

This document reviews the "fun factor" of the 10 playtests, focusing on the feedback of the five playtesting agents (Cyrus, Arthur, Valerie, Billy, Thorne/Kaelen/Volt/Flora/Ignis). It addresses the critical balance between mechanical risk and narrative agency, focusing on how to make death feel like a consequence rather than an accidental byproduct of poor dice rolls.

---

## 1. Fun Level Evaluation (Agent Feedback)

### The "High Fun" Sessions (Playtests 1, 3, 7, 8, 9)
These sessions were described by all player agents as **highly engaging, tactical, and narrative-first**.
* **Valerie (Expert Player):** *"Defensive posture felt amazing. Intercepting a strike to save Pippa or Sophia made me feel like an active guardian rather than a passive target. I had meaningful tactical choices every exchange."*
* **Billy (Novice Player):** *"I loved spending Sparks for advantage rolls when the stakes were high. Rolling 3d6 on a hard check and succeeding felt like a true movie moment."*
* **Cyrus (Expert MM):** *"Resolving combat simultaneously kept the pacing fast. There was no downtime. Adjudicating partial successes (7-9) allowed me to create shifting hazards that players could react to immediately."*

### The "Zero Fun" TPK Sessions (Playtests 4, 10)
These sessions resulted in Total Party Kills and were described as **frustrating, dry, and mathematically oppressive**.
* **Billy (Novice Player):** *"It wasn't fun at all. The magma/steam was ticking for damage every turn. I didn't get to do anything cool or make choices; I was just rolling saves to not die, and then I ran out of Endurance."*
* **Arthur (Novice MM):** *"I felt like an executioner, not a GM. The rules forced me to apply environmental damage constantly, and because the players rolled poorly, they died without any heroic options."*
* **Valerie (Expert Player):** *"This is the 'math trap.' When passive environmental damage is combined with TR 9 boss attacks, the damage output exceeds the party's mitigation limit. Death felt accidental and cheap, not earned."*

---

## 2. The Core Problem: Accidental Death vs. Narrative Consequence

In *Facets of Origin*, character death should be a significant narrative event. However, under the current rules, **death is a math problem**. 
If a player rolls consecutive failures on standard dodge/parry checks while dealing with active hazards, their Endurance pool is reduced to 0, resulting in immediate defeat or death. This feels accidental and frustrating because:
1. It occurs due to bad dice luck rather than a bad decision.
2. It completely strips the player of narrative agency at the most critical moment of their character's story.

---

## 3. Revisions to Enhance Fun and Agency

To resolve this, we propose four ruleset revisions to ensure that risk feels like a choice, and death only occurs as an intentional consequence.

### Revision 1: The "Doom Gate" (Death as a Choice)
*   **The Rule:** When a character takes damage that would reduce them below 0 Endurance, they do not die or pass out. Instead, they remain active at 0 Endurance and gain the **Doom Gate** condition.
*   **The Consequence:** The next time the character takes damage or fails a physical roll while at 0 Endurance, the player must choose one of two options:
    1.  **Suffer a Permanent Scar/Disability:** Agree to lose an eye, shatter a limb, or destroy a magical focus. The damage is ignored, they stay in the fight, but they carry a permanent mechanical penalty.
    2.  **Accept a Heroic Death:** The character dies in this exchange, but they get to narrate a final heroic action that automatically succeeds with **Critical Success** (equivalent to rolling a 12), bypassing all enemy armor and immunities to aid their allies.
*   **Fun Impact:** This completely eliminates accidental deaths. If a character dies, it is because the player *chose* to make a heroic sacrifice to save the team, turning a mechanical failure into a legendary story moment.

### Revision 2: Telegraphed "Threat Clocks" for Hazards
*   **The Rule:** Environmental hazards (lava, steam, falling rocks) no longer deal passive, automatic damage every turn. Instead, they operate on a **Threat Clock** (a 4-segment visual tracker).
*   **The Mechanics:** 
    *   The MM advances the clock by 1 segment on a player's partial success (7-9) or failure (6-).
    *   When the clock fills, the hazard strikes.
    *   Players can spend their action in Measured posture to make a check (e.g. Finesse to turn a valve, Warding to freeze lava) to reduce the clock by 1-2 segments.
*   **Fun Impact:** This transforms hazards from dry "damage taxes" into interactive puzzle elements that players can actively mitigate.

### Revision 3: The "Catch Breath" Posture Maneuver
*   **The Rule:** Add a new reaction/maneuver available in the **Withdrawn** posture.
*   **The Mechanics:** If a player chooses the Withdrawn posture and spends their exchange performing no attacks or support actions, they can **Catch Breath** to restore 1 Endurance or clear one Tier 1 physical condition (like Singed or Nauseated).
*   **Fun Impact:** Gives players an emergency "fallback option" when their resources are dangerously low, increasing tactical depth.

### Revision 4: Spark Refund on Volatile Magic Fails
*   **The Rule:** If a pre-technique spellcaster spends a Spark to roll 3d6 on a magic cast, and the roll still results in a failure (6-), the **Spark is immediately refunded**.
*   **Fun Impact:** Mitigates the high volatility of early-game magic casting. Players will feel more comfortable attempting creative spells without the fear of permanently wasting their limited Spark safety nets.
