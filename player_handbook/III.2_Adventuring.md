# Rules: Adventuring

This chapter covers three things: the pressure of hazards outside combat, how characters recover from harm, and what happens when a character's life is on the line. It does not cover travel, downtime, or exploration procedures — those stay out of scope for the core rules. Everything here still resolves on the same 2d6 system from Chapter III.1. No new dice mechanic is introduced anywhere in this chapter.

---

## Hazards and Threat Clocks

Not every danger is an enemy who takes a turn. Rising water, a collapsing ceiling, a spreading fire, a ritual counting down to completion — these are hazards, and they need a way to apply pressure without inventing a new subsystem for each one.

A **Threat Clock** is a four-segment tracker, visible to the whole table, that represents a hazard closing in.

- **It advances** one segment whenever a character rolls a **partial success (7–9) or a failure (6-)** on a roll made near the hazard. A full success (10+) never advances it — the character handled the situation cleanly enough that the hazard gained no ground.
- **When it fills**, the hazard strikes: the MM applies a Condition to those caught in it, or calls for a forced roll against a difficulty the MM states before the roll.
- **Winding it back** costs a character an action, nothing more. There is no roll. A player who spends their action shoring up the failing wall, or working the valve that vents the pressure, winds the clock back one segment automatically.

**Why the wind-back never rolls.** If winding the clock back required a roll, a 7–9 on that very roll would advance the clock you are trying to wind back — a rules-lawyer loop, and an unkind trap for the table in the one chapter written to keep hazards simple for a novice MM to run. Spending the action is the cost. It always works.

**The pacing is deliberate.** Roughly 72% of rolls made near a hazard land on partial success or failure — that is simply how often a roll clears 6 or misses 10 across the game's normal range of modifiers. A 4-segment clock, at that rate, fills in roughly 3–4 party rolls. That is the intended feel: hazards should close in fast enough to matter within a single scene, not linger as background noise. If a hazard is dragging on past that window, the MM should either resolve it narratively or let it fill.

No new resolution mechanic — the clock advances off the outcome tiers you already know from Chapter III.1. Multiple hazards can run at once, each on its own clock.

## In Play: The Mill at Millhaven

*The investigation has finally led out of Thornwall and up the valley to Millhaven itself — to the mill the village is named for, which has not ground grain in fifteen years, and whose undercroft is where the trail ends.*

---

**MM:** "The undercroft is low, stone, and older than the mill above it. Floor grates along the west wall. A sluice gate in the corner, held shut by a winch and chain. A rack of rusted tools by the stairs. And the door you came through, which has swollen into its frame behind you in a way you are choosing not to think about. As Mordai steps off the stairs, the winch — which has been holding back the millpond since before any of you were born — snaps."

(A pause, for effect.)

**MM:** "Water starts coming up through the grates. This is a hazard, not a fight — I'm putting a four-segment Threat Clock on the table. When it fills, the water wins."

**Zahna:** "A millrace this old will have a relief valve to bleed the pond. I look for it."

**MM:** "Standard. Roll Intelligence."

→ Zahna rolls **2d6 + Intelligence (3 → +1)** at Standard difficulty and gets an **8**. Partial success.

**MM:** "There is a relief valve — you can see the housing on the east wall, behind a collapsed shelf of quern-stones. But the finding took time you did not have. The water is over your boots." *One segment fills.*

**Mordai:** "I move the stones."

→ Mordai rolls **2d6 + Strength (3 → +1)** at Standard difficulty and gets a **7**. Partial success.

**MM:** "You move the stones. You also move the shelf, which turns out to have been load-bearing in a way nobody appreciated until now. The valve is clear — and the water is at your knees." *Two of four.*

**Zulnut:** "I'm not rolling anything. I take a pry bar off that tool rack and spend my whole action wedging the biggest floor grate shut."

**MM:** "That's winding the clock back — it costs your action and nothing else. No roll. It just works. The inflow chokes to a trickle." *The clock ticks back to one.*

**Zulnut:** "The best kind of action. The kind that can't fail."

*It cannot fail. It also cannot last. The valve wheel turns out to be seized — a failure, and the second segment fills. The pry bar shears — a partial, three. And Mordai's shoulder, applied directly to the problem, opens the valve and tears the housing half off the wall in the same motion — a partial, four. The clock fills.*

**MM:** "The pond finds the gap where the housing was. The surge takes all three of you off your feet — that's the hazard striking: **Winded**, everyone, Tier 1. The good news is the water now has somewhere to go that isn't this room. The bad news is the sound from directly overhead — the main beam under the mill floor has been soaking all scene, and it has just cracked like a mast."

(The MM looks at the map, then at the party, with the expression of someone doing load calculations.)

## Getting Hurt and Getting Better

Combat Conditions (Chapter III.3) work the same way outside a fight — a fall, a trap, a hazard filling its clock, a hostile crowd all apply Conditions exactly as a Strike would. Recovery works the same regardless of where the Condition came from:

- **Tier 1** (Winded, Off-Balance, Shaken) clears automatically at the end of the exchange in combat; outside combat, where no exchange structure exists, it clears at the end of the scene it was applied in.
- **Tier 2** (Staggered, Cornered) persists until **treated**. Treated means one of three things: a scene passes in which the character has a genuine chance to recover (resting, regrouping, tending to the injury), a successful roll on a skill relevant to the harm (Endurance to push through it, a relevant Facet skill to address its cause), or downtime the MM rules is sufficient. The MM decides which applies based on the fiction — a Cornered character needs to actually get out of the corner; a Staggered one needs a moment to recover their footing.
- **Tier 3 — Broken** — clears at the end of the scene. The character is out of that conflict, but not out of the story. Meaningful recovery from Broken — full physical or narrative recuperation — takes real downtime, not a single scene.

None of this changes between a fight and a hazard. A character Staggered by a collapsing bridge recovers exactly like one Staggered by a blade.

---

## When a Character Would Die

**Broken never kills you.** Reaching Tier 3 means a character is out of the current conflict — defeated, unconscious, fled, captured, or collapsed, whatever the fiction calls for — but alive, by default, always.

There is exactly one moment where death enters the game: when a Broken result would end a character's life *in the fiction* — falling from a Boss with a killing blow already narrated, the ritual completing with them still inside it, the fire reaching them before anyone can pull them out. When that moment arrives, the choice is the player's and no one else's:

- **Take a permanent scar.** A lasting cost that the player and the MM name together — a lost eye, a limb that never works the same, a broken magical focus, a fear that never fully leaves. The character stays down, alive, and carries the scar forward.
- **Choose a heroic death.** The character takes one final action, and it automatically succeeds — no roll, no difficulty, no failure state. Whatever they attempt to do with that last action, it lands. Then the character dies.

Both options are the player's to choose, never the MM's to impose. This is what makes death in this game a story beat instead of an accident: if a character dies, it is because the player chose the ending, not because a bad roll ended it for them.

## In Play: The Beam

*The valve is open, the pond is draining through the undercroft instead of into it, and the party is scrambling for the stairs. The main beam goes just as Zahna reaches the door. Mordai does what Mordai does: he steps under it.*

---

**MM:** "You catch it. Both hands, full squat, the wet weight of a mill floor across your shoulders — and it buys Zahna and Zulnut the doorway. I'm restarting the water's clock, and you are still standing in the hazard. It advances on Zahna's scramble up the millrace — a partial. It advances on Zulnut's. It fills on the beam itself settling deeper. The hazard strikes the one person still in it, and given what you are holding and where you are holding it: Mordai, you're **Broken**."

**MM:** "And I want to be precise here, because this is the one moment in the game where precision matters most. Broken doesn't kill you — ever. But the fiction right now is a man alone under a failing beam with the water at his chest, and if it comes down with him under it, that is the end. So the rules stop being mine and become yours. Two options. You take a permanent scar — we name it together, and Mordai lives. Or you choose a heroic death: one final action, anything you can describe, and it succeeds automatically — no roll, no difficulty. And then Mordai's story ends."

(The table has gone very quiet. Zulnut's player has put down the snacks.)

**Mordai:** "Are they clear?"

**MM:** "Zahna's through. Zulnut is on the threshold, looking back at you."

**Mordai:** "Then nobody needs the heroic version. I drop the beam, I dive for the millrace, and I take the scar. The shoulder — the one that held it. Mordai's carried every heavy thing this party ever found. Now there are things he can't."

**MM:** "The shoulder sets wrong and stays that way. Cold mornings, it announces the weather. That's the scar — written down, permanent, true."

*Mordai comes out of the millrace downstream, alive, coughing. The mill floor comes down behind him. The scar goes on the sheet, and from now on it is simply a fact of the world: the strongest back in the party has one thing it will never carry again.*

**Zulnut:** "For the record, I'd have taken the heroic death. Extremely dramatic. No cleanup."

**MM:** (Nobody doubts it, Zulnut.)
