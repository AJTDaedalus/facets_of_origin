# Compendium: Equipment

## Philosophy

Equipment in Facets of Origin is narrative, not numerical. A weapon is not a stat block — it is a tool that shapes how your character engages with the world and what kind of stories they create. The system cares about what your equipment *means* for the fiction, not how many decimal points it contributes to your roll.

---

## Weapons

Weapons determine which attribute you use for a Strike. Choose the weapon that fits your character's fiction.

| Category | Attribute | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Heavy** | Strength | Greatsword, warhammer, greataxe, maul |
| **Standard** | Strength or Dexterity (player's choice) | Longsword, mace, spear, staff, hand axe |
| **Light** | Dexterity | Dagger, short sword, rapier, hand crossbow |
| **Ranged** | Dexterity | Longbow, crossbow, sling, thrown weapons |
| **Unarmed** | Strength or Dexterity (player's choice) | Fists, grappling, martial arts, headbutts |

All weapons deal the same mechanical effect: a successful Strike applies a Condition of the appropriate tier. There are no damage dice, no weapon damage tables, no +1 swords in the base rules. The fiction determines what the hit looks like; the tier system determines the mechanical consequence.

> **Why no damage numbers?** Because tracking variable damage per weapon creates complexity without meaningful choice in a Condition-based system. A dagger and a greatsword both apply Staggered on a 10+ — the fiction of *how* they do it is different, and that difference matters narratively, not arithmetically.

### Weapon Specialization

Characters with relevant Techniques (such as Weapon Mastery from the Body Facet tree) may gain additional benefits with specific weapon types. These are handled through the Technique system, not through weapon stat blocks.

## Armor

Armor reduces the severity of incoming Conditions. It does not add to your rolls or create a separate defense value.

| Armor | Effect | Fictional Weight |
|---|---|---|
| **None** | Conditions apply at full tier | Unencumbered, silent, mobile |
| **Light** | Tier 2 Conditions become Tier 1 | Leather, padded, chain shirt — some noise, some weight |
| **Heavy** | Tier 3 (Broken) becomes Tier 2 | Plate, full chain, heavy brigandine — loud, hot, imposing |

Light armor softens serious blows into glancing ones. Heavy armor keeps you standing when an unarmored character would be Broken — but you are still Staggered and hurting.

### Armor in the Fiction

Armor has consequences beyond combat:

- **Light armor** is unobtrusive. It can be concealed under clothing. It does not meaningfully affect Stealth, social situations, or movement.
- **Heavy armor** announces your presence. It imposes noise, heat, and conspicuousness. The MM may increase difficulty on Stealth, Finesse, or social checks where heavy armor is a liability. It is not subtle, and it is not comfortable. It is, however, extremely good at keeping you in the fight.

## Adventuring Gear

Adventuring gear is handled narratively rather than as an itemized inventory. If your character would reasonably have a piece of common equipment given their background and preparation, they have it. If it is unusual, specialized, or the MM decides supply matters in this moment, it becomes a fiction question.

### Common Gear (assumed available)

If your character is an adventurer who has had time to prepare, they carry:

- A weapon appropriate to their background
- A light source (torch, lantern, candle)
- Rope (50 feet is the standard TTRPG unit of rope, and we see no reason to change this)
- Basic rations and a waterskin
- A pack or satchel
- Tools of their trade (lockpicks for a thief, a journal for a scholar, a holy symbol for a priest)

### Specialized Gear

Items that are unusual, expensive, or narratively significant are tracked individually. These include:

- **Healing supplies** — bandages, poultices, or alchemical aids that allow treating Tier 2 Conditions outside of combat
- **Specialty tools** — a disguise kit, an alchemist's kit, cartographer's tools. Having the right tools may reduce the difficulty of relevant skill checks by one step
- **Magical items** — covered in a future chapter; these have specific, named effects

### The "Do You Have It?" Rule

When a player needs a piece of common gear not on their character sheet:

1. Is it reasonable that this character would carry it? (A former soldier probably has a whetstone. A scholar probably has ink.)
2. Did the character have time to prepare before the current situation?

If both answers are yes, they have it. If either is uncertain, the MM may call for a Luck roll — 10+ means you thought ahead, 7–9 means you have something close but not quite right, 6- means you definitely do not have it and the absence matters.

## Currency and Trade

The base rules do not include a detailed economy system. Currency exists in the fiction — characters can buy, sell, and trade — but prices, exchange rates, and wealth tracking are left to the MM's judgment and the needs of the story.

> **MM Guidance:** If money matters in your campaign, establish a simple framework at session zero: are the characters poor, comfortable, or wealthy? What can they afford without rolling, and what requires effort? Keep it narrative. The Economy Facet module (planned) provides detailed rules for campaigns where trade, commerce, and resource management are central to the story.
