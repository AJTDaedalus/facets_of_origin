# Character Creation: Facet of the Mind

## The Mind Facet

Characters of the Mind Facet solve problems by understanding them. They are scholars and detectives, navigators and arcane theorists, the person who notices the detail no one else caught and the person who already knows what that detail means. Where a Body character reshapes the world through presence and force, a Mind character reshapes it through perception, knowledge, and the quiet advantage of being correct before anyone else realizes the question was asked.

Their tools are attention, memory, and analysis. Their superpower is that they are almost never the most surprised person in the room.

---

## Skills of the Mind

| Skill | Attribute | Covers |
|---|---|---|
| **Lore** | Knowledge | Recalling and applying accumulated learning: history, cultures, languages, creature lore, arcane theory, religious traditions, and the significance of what you find |
| **Investigate** | Intelligence | Active analysis: examining a scene, finding patterns, working out how something functions, deducing what happened, identifying the thing that doesn't fit |
| **Craft** | Intelligence | Making, repairing, and modifying objects: tools, mechanisms, structures, alchemical preparations, and anything that requires precision of thought as much as precision of hand |
| **Insight** | Wisdom | Reading people and situations: understanding what someone actually wants, sensing deception, noticing the wrong note in a conversation before you can name it |
| **Survival** | Wisdom | Navigating and enduring unfamiliar environments: tracking, finding food and shelter, reading weather and terrain, sensing danger in the wild before it arrives |

*(Skills of the Body and Soul are defined in their respective Facet chapters.)*

## Facet of the Mind — Technique Tree

The Mind tree has three branches: **Clarity** (Intelligence), **Instinct** (Wisdom), and **Archive** (Knowledge).

---

### Clarity Branch

Clarity Techniques represent the trained precision of a disciplined mind — the ability to take a situation apart, find the load-bearing piece, and know exactly what to do with it.

---

#### Tier 1

**Sharp Analysis** *(Intelligence)*
When you spend time examining an object, mechanism, or scene — at least a few focused minutes — you may ask the MM one question about it that must be answered honestly. *What holds this together? What is this for? What happened here?* The MM cannot deflect with "you're not sure." You spent the time. You get an answer. *(The question must concern something your character can directly observe or has directly interacted with — not speculation about future events or the inner lives of people you haven't met.)*

**Dissecting Failure** *(Intelligence)*
When you fail an Investigate roll, you still learn one true fact about what you were examining. Your analysis fell short — but your mind does not come up entirely empty. The MM chooses what you learn; it is always real, and it always matters.

---

#### Tier 2 *(requires one Clarity Tier 1)*

**Structural Weakness**
Once per scene, when you have had the chance to observe a plan, structure, mechanism, or system in action, you may declare one specific flaw or vulnerability within it. The MM must confirm the flaw exists and describe what exploiting it would require. You cannot invent a weakness that isn't there — but if one is there, you will find it.

**Working Theory**
Once per scene, after you have gathered at least one piece of evidence about a situation, you may state a conclusion about it as a theory. The MM tells you whether your theory is *correct*, *partially correct*, or *wrong* — and if wrong, gives you the one fact that most directly contradicts it. You do not need to have all the evidence yet. You just need enough to reason.

---

#### Tier 3 *(requires one Clarity Tier 2)*

**The Deduction**
Once per session, you may make a reasoned deduction about a situation, location, or person you have not directly observed — working only from second-hand information, physical evidence, or inference. State your conclusion clearly. The MM tells you whether it is correct. If it is, everything that follows in the fiction treats your conclusion as established fact.

**Pressure Point**
Once per session, when you have identified a specific weakness, flaw, or vulnerability in an obstacle (using Structural Weakness or by other means), you may describe exactly how to exploit it. The MM must treat this as the optimal approach — the difficulty of acting on your analysis is one step easier for any character who follows your instructions, for the rest of the scene.

### Instinct Branch

Instinct Techniques represent a mind so attuned to pattern and environment that it operates ahead of conscious thought — the gut sense that something is wrong, the read on a room that arrives before you've identified what you're reading.

---

#### Tier 1

**Never Surprised** *(Wisdom)*
You are not caught off guard. If the MM would call for a roll to notice an ambush, trap, or sudden threat before it lands, you automatically succeed. You may not always be able to prevent what's coming — but you always see it coming.

**The Wrong Note** *(Wisdom)*
When something in your environment is deliberately concealed, falsified, or out of place, the MM will tell you that something feels off — even if they do not tell you what. This is not a roll. You simply notice the dissonance. What you do with that feeling is still yours to decide.

---

#### Tier 2 *(requires one Instinct Tier 1)*

**Immediate Threat**
Once per scene, you may ask the MM: *"What is the most immediate danger here?"* They must answer honestly and specifically. Not *"the room feels dangerous"* — the actual threat, named. You can act on this information as if you had observed it directly.

**Assess the Situation**
Once per scene, when you enter a social situation — a negotiation, a gathering, a tense confrontation — you may ask the MM two questions about the emotional or political dynamics in the room. *Who holds the real power here? Who is afraid? What does this person actually want?* Both answers must be honest.

---

#### Tier 3 *(requires one Instinct Tier 2)*

**First Move**
Once per session, your instincts extend to everyone around you. You may declare that the party acts on your read of the situation — before any opposition can react, before any ambush springs, before the moment tips. Everyone in the party acts first in the next exchange. No roll required.

**Pattern Recognition**
Once per session, you may name a pattern you have observed across multiple scenes or sessions — a repeated behavior, a structural similarity, a tell that keeps appearing. The MM must confirm whether the pattern is real. If it is, you understand something about the situation, the antagonist, or the world that no amount of direct investigation could have uncovered. The MM tells you one true implication of that pattern that the fiction has not yet revealed.

### Archive Branch

Archive Techniques represent the depth of accumulated learning — not just knowing a lot, but knowing *the right thing* at *the right moment*, and understanding how to bring that knowledge to bear when it matters.

---

#### Tier 1

**Field of Mastery** *(Knowledge)*
Choose one field of knowledge: history, arcane theory, natural sciences, theology, law, languages, geography, or another domain with MM approval. Lore rolls that fall within your chosen field are treated as one difficulty step easier. You are not broadly competent here — you are genuinely expert, and the fiction should reflect it.

**Arcane Study** *(Knowledge)*
You have developed a formal magical domain through scholarly practice. Choose one domain from the Domains of the Mind list (Chapter II.3). You may use magic within that domain using the Domain + Intent + Scope framework. Roll Knowledge when doing so.

---

#### Tier 2 *(requires one Archive Tier 1)*

**First Encounter**
When you encounter something for the first time — a creature, a location, a historical figure, an artifact, a faction — you may ask the MM two questions about it that must be answered truthfully. *What is its weakness? What does it want? What is its origin? What does this symbol mean?* The answers represent what a scholar of your depth would already know. You do not need to have met this thing before. You just need to have read the right things.

**Cross-Reference**
Once per scene, when you are presented with a new piece of information, you may immediately connect it to something you already know. Tell the MM what you think the connection is. They must tell you whether you are right — and if you are, describe one additional implication that the connection reveals.

---

#### Tier 3 *(requires one Archive Tier 2)*

**The Knowledge That Saves**
Once per session, your depth of knowledge prevents a consequence the party would otherwise face. Name what you know — the compound's weakness to salt water, the governor's standing debt to this guild, the creature's aversion to open flame, the treaty clause that voids the arrest warrant — and the MM confirms whether it applies. If it applies, the consequence is negated. You do not roll. You simply know the thing that matters, because you have always been the kind of person who reads everything.

**Deep Archive**
Once per session, you may recall something you have no direct reason to know — a piece of esoteric knowledge from a tradition you weren't trained in, a fact from a period of history outside your specialty, a technical detail from a craft you've never practiced. Describe what you're reaching for. The MM tells you whether your character, given their depth of study and their habit of reading broadly and well, could plausibly have encountered this knowledge. If yes: you know it, and it is accurate.
