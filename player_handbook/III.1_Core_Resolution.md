# Rules: Core Resolution

## The 2d6 System

When the outcome of an action is uncertain and the stakes matter, you roll **2d6 + the relevant modifier**. The total determines the outcome.

| Roll Total | Outcome |
|---|---|
| **10+** | **Full success** — you achieve your goal cleanly |
| **7–9** | **Success with a cost** — you succeed, but with a complication or consequence |
| **6 or less** | **Things go wrong** — the story always moves forward, but not in your favor |

This is the only resolution mechanic in the game. Combat, magic, exploration, social encounters — everything resolves on this same three-tier system. Learn it once, use it everywhere.

---

## Modifiers

Your roll total is **2d6 + attribute modifier + skill modifier + difficulty modifier**. Each component:

### Attribute Modifier

The Minor Attribute most relevant to what you are attempting provides the modifier. See Chapter II.2 for the full attribute list.

| Rating | Label | Modifier |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Weak | -1 |
| 2 | Average | +0 |
| 3 | Strong | +1 |

**Major Attributes** are used when no single Minor Attribute clearly fits, or for **saving throws** (see below).

### Skill Modifier

If you have a relevant skill, its rank provides an additional modifier:

| Rank | Modifier |
|---|---|
| Novice | +0 |
| Practiced | +1 |
| Expert | +2 |
| Master | +3 |

Skills are optional on any roll. You always roll with an attribute; you add a skill when your training directly applies.

### Difficulty

Before calling for a roll, the MM declares a **difficulty** — a statement about the circumstances, not a judgment about your character.

| Difficulty | Modifier | When |
|---|---|---|
| Easy | +1 | Clear advantage, weak opposition, favorable conditions |
| Standard | +0 | The default — uncertain outcome, no special advantage |
| Hard | -1 | Skilled opposition, poor conditions, acting against the grain |
| Very Hard | -2 | Extraordinary opposition, severe circumstances, nearly impossible odds |

The MM declares difficulty **before** you roll.

## Sparks

A **Spark** is a narrative resource you earn by doing something remarkable — and spend for an edge when the stakes are highest. Each character begins each session with **3 Sparks**.

### Earning Sparks

Sparks are awarded for moments that make the table sit up. They can be awarded at any moment — mid-combat, mid-scene, immediately when the moment happens.

- **MM award:** Exceptional roleplay, creative problem-solving, or playing your character's established traits and weaknesses at real cost.
- **"Spark?" — the peer call:** Any player can call out "Spark?" for *another* player's moment. The MM confirms. The moment is recognized by the table collectively.
- **The Graceful Fail:** When your roll is a 6- and you lean into the consequence — making the failure richer — the MM may award a Spark.

### Spending Sparks

Before you roll, you may spend one or more Sparks. Each Spark **adds a d6** to your roll; you then **drop an equal number of lowest dice**. The base remains 2d6 — Sparks shift the odds by replacing your worst potential outcomes.

> *One Spark: roll 3d6, drop the lowest. Two Sparks: roll 4d6, drop the two lowest.*

Sparks are most useful when everything is on the line. A Spark spent on a trivial roll is a Spark not available at the moment that matters. That tension is the point.

## Saving Throws

When something happens *to* your character — not something they choose to attempt — the MM calls for a **saving throw**. You roll 2d6 + the relevant **Major Attribute modifier**.

Saving throws use Major Attributes because the forces acting on your character are broad:

- **Body:** Resisting a toxin, surviving a collapse, enduring extreme cold, holding your footing against a shockwave
- **Mind:** Resisting an illusion, holding concentration under pressure, recognizing magical compulsion, keeping your composure when reality shifts
- **Soul:** Resisting domination, holding your identity against corruption, enduring grief or supernatural terror, standing firm against a force of will

The outcome tiers are the same as any other roll:
- **10+:** You resist completely
- **7–9:** You resist, but with a cost — partial exposure, momentary lapse, lingering effect
- **6-:** The effect takes hold — the MM describes the consequence

> **When to call for a saving throw vs. a skill check:** A saving throw is reactive — something is acting on the character. A skill check is proactive — the character is acting on the world. If the character chose to engage with the threat, it is a skill check. If the threat came to them, it is a saving throw.

## Contested Rolls

When you act against someone actively working against you:

**Against an NPC:** Only you roll. The NPC's relevant attribute informs the difficulty the MM selects — a perceptive guard makes your Stealth roll Hard; an oblivious one makes it Easy. NPCs do not roll dice.

**Against another player character:** Both players roll. The higher total wins the contest. On a tie, both achieve partial success — neither prevails cleanly, and the situation deepens.

---

## When Not to Roll

The MM should only call for a roll when three conditions are met:

1. **The outcome is uncertain.** If success is guaranteed or failure is impossible given the fiction, there is no roll. A professional lockpick does not roll to open a simple lock with proper tools and no pressure. A character with Strength 1 cannot lift a portcullis alone regardless of what they roll.

2. **The stakes matter.** Something must change depending on the outcome. If nothing interesting happens on a failure, there is no roll — just narrate the success and move on.

3. **Both success and failure move the story forward.** A 6- should never stop the story. If you cannot think of an interesting consequence for failure, the roll should not happen.

> **Sidebar: The "Don't Roll, Just Do It" Principle**
>
> Facets has fewer rolls per session than most TTRPGs. This is intentional. Every roll should feel like it matters — a moment of genuine tension where the table leans in. If you are rolling for routine actions, mundane conversations, or situations where the outcome is already determined by the fiction, the rolls are stealing weight from the moments that earn it.
>
> The MM's default should lean toward *yes* — let the character succeed, let the story move, and save the dice for the moments that crackle.

## Outcome Guidance

### Full Success (10+)

You get what you wanted, cleanly. The MM should not add hidden costs, surprise complications, or "yes, but" qualifiers to a 10+. The player earned this.

### Partial Success (7–9)

You achieved something, but not cleanly. The MM must name the cost *before* the player decides how to proceed. Useful patterns:

- *Social:* You get the agreement, but the source knows you wanted it badly
- *Exploration:* You find what you sought, but something finds you in the process
- *Investigation:* You learn one true fact, but the conclusion you draw is yours to verify
- *Physical:* The task succeeds, but it cost something — time, equipment, noise, position

The complication should always move the story forward.

### Failure (6-)

Something *develops* — usually in a direction nobody planned. The story always moves forward. The MM's job on a 6- is not to punish the player but to complicate the situation.

- *You get what you wanted — but so does something else*
- *The attempt reveals something unexpected*
- *The action works partially, with a cost*
- *The moment is real but complicated*

A 6- is the moment for the **Graceful Fail** — leaning into the consequence or adding something to the fiction that makes the failure richer. The MM may award a Spark for this.
