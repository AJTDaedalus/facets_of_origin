# Playtest Report
## "The Thornwall Undercroft" — Facets of Origin v0.1

**Session:** 1 session, ~2.5 hours simulated
**Party:** 3 PCs (Body/Mind/Soul), all career_advances 1, Party Strength 3
**Encounters:** Skirmish (3 Mook dust constructs, TR 3) + Standard (Named NPC Guardian, TR 8)
**Total Rolls:** 33 (7 full success, 17 partial, 9 failure)
**Sparks Spent:** 2 of 9 available (both by Mira on magic)
**Magic Used:** 5 times (2 Inscription, 3 Resonance) — 2 full success, 1 partial, 2 failure

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The system works. Character creation was fast (~15 minutes per character), the session flowed naturally between social/exploration/combat scenes, and the three-tier outcome system (10+/7-9/6-) produced consistently interesting narrative beats. Combat was quick (2-3 exchanges per fight, ~10-20 minutes real time) and tactically engaging without becoming a miniatures game.

The biggest strengths: speed of play, meaningful decisions at every roll, and the way the condition/endurance system creates tension without HP attrition. The biggest concerns: some ambiguous NPC combat interactions, pre-technique magic feeling too punishing for Standard-type domains, and the Spark economy being under-used because players hoarded them.

---

## WHAT WORKED

### 1. Three-Tier Outcomes Are the Engine

Every roll produced a story beat. The 17 partial successes (52% of rolls) were the stars — each one forced a decision or created a complication that drove the scene forward. Highlights:

- **Roll #1 (Mira's Insight on Brost):** Partial revealed *something* was off without giving everything away. Created a thread the players pulled on all session.
- **Roll #5 (Kael's snake eyes on the lock):** Failure broke the key inside the lock, escalating the problem. The players unanimously praised this as better than "the door doesn't open."
- **Roll #11 (Kael's Strike on Mook):** Partial success still killed the Mook (any success does) but the "cost" was the remaining Mook repositioning. Partial success against Mooks works elegantly.

The 6- results consistently moved the story forward rather than halting it. No roll produced a dead end. This is the PbtA DNA working as intended.

### 2. Combat Speed and Tactical Depth

The Mook fight took 2 exchanges (~10 minutes). The Guardian fight took 3 exchanges (~20 minutes). Neither felt rushed or dragged. Key factors:

- **Simultaneous posture declaration** created genuine tension. Alex couldn't wait to see what the Guardian did before committing to Aggressive. Posture choices had immediate mechanical consequences (Aggressive +1 offense but +1 reaction cost nearly killed Kael in Exchange 2).
- **Conditions instead of HP** made damage feel narrative, not numerical. "Staggered" tells a story that "-8 HP" doesn't. The escalation from Tier 1 → Tier 2 → Broken created a clear combat arc.
- **Lateral solutions** rewarded investigation. Vessa's Maneuver to find the ward-lantern weakness changed the fight's trajectory. The Guardian's phase change raised stakes at the right moment.
- **The Intercept** was a standout moment. Kael spent 4 Endurance (Intercept 2 + Parry 2 at Aggressive) to protect Mira — mechanically expensive, narratively powerful.

### 3. Character Differentiation at Level 0

Despite starting with only a +1 skill bonus and +1 attribute modifier at best, all three characters felt distinct:

- **Kael** dominated combat (Str+1, Combat+1, light armor) but struggled with knowledge rolls (Int -1, no Lore skill).
- **Vessa** excelled at investigation (Int+1, Lore+1) and used Inscription magic creatively, but was physically fragile (Str -1, 4 End, no armor).
- **Mira** was the social anchor (Cha+1, Spirit+1) and used Resonance magic for utility, but her pre-technique magic was unreliable and she had no combat capability.

The Background Specialty system shone. Kael's Watch Specialty provided key information (challenge-response protocol, city construct knowledge) without requiring high Knowledge scores. It rewarded character backstory mechanically.

### 4. Magic as Creative Problem-Solving

The Domain + Intent + Scope framework produced five distinct magical effects from just two domains:

1. **Resonance: feel the vibrations in the door inscription** (utility sensing)
2. **Inscription: flash of disorienting light on a Mook** (combat offense)
3. **Resonance: disrupt the Guardian's harmonic frequency** (combat offense — failed)
4. **Inscription: overload the ward-lantern's inscription** (combat utility — failed)
5. **Resonance: shatter the ward-lantern with pitched vibration** (combat utility)

No two uses were alike, and none resembled "I cast Fireball." The system encouraged creative interpretation of a thematic domain rather than picking from a spell list. Sam and Riley both noted this was exciting but required MM collaboration — the player proposes, the MM adjudicates scope and difficulty.

### 5. Session Pacing and Structure

The three-act framework from MM2 worked naturally:
- **Act I (Hook):** Social scene with Brost, information gathering with Lira. ~15 minutes, 2 rolls.
- **Act II (Rising Action):** Exploration, investigation, sealed door, Mook fight, Guardian discovery. ~60 minutes, 20 rolls.
- **Act III (Climax):** Guardian fight and vault discovery. ~30 minutes, 11 rolls.

Scene transitions were smooth. The system didn't impose scene boundaries — the MM moved between social, exploration, and combat organically. The dice were called only when outcome was uncertain and interesting, consistent with the "three conditions for rolling" from MM2.

## WHAT DIDN'T WORK (OR NEEDS CLARIFICATION)

### 1. NPC Attack Resolution Is Ambiguous

Three separate rules questions arose during combat about how NPC attacks interact with the system:

**a) NPC offense modifier vs. reaction difficulty:**
When the Guardian went Aggressive (+1 offense), the table wasn't sure how this affected the PC's reaction roll. The MM ruled that NPC offense modifiers adjust reaction difficulty, but this isn't explicitly stated in the PHB.

**Recommendation:** Add a sentence to III.3 Combat: "When an NPC declares a posture with an offense modifier, that modifier adjusts the difficulty of reactions against their attacks. Aggressive NPC = reactions one step harder. Defensive NPC = reactions one step easier."

**b) Absorb against Named NPCs — what tier?**
When Kael Absorbed the Guardian's attack, the table debated whether a Named NPC's "full hit" is Tier 1 or Tier 2. The MM ruled Tier 2 (matching a successful Strike's full success outcome).

**Recommendation:** Add to the Absorb description: "When you Absorb an attack from a Named NPC or Boss, the incoming Condition is Tier 2 (the default for a successful offensive action). Absorbing a Mook's attack is Tier 1."

**c) Armor + Parry partial stacking:**
Light armor downgrades Tier 2 → Tier 1. Parry partial downgrades one tier. Do they stack? The MM ruled they don't (take the better reduction). This seems right but isn't specified.

**Recommendation:** Add a note: "Armor and reaction downgrades do not stack. Apply the greater reduction."

### 2. Pre-Technique Magic Penalty Is Too Harsh for Standard/Broad Domains

Mira's Resonance (Standard type) with pre-technique penalty:
- Minor scope: Standard → Hard (-1)
- Effective roll: 2d6 + Spirit(+1) + Hard(-1) = 2d6+0

This gives a **42% failure rate** on the simplest possible magical effect. Mira failed 2 of 3 Resonance attempts (66% failure rate in practice — small sample, but the math supports the frustration).

Compare Vessa's Inscription (Focused type):
- Minor scope: Easy → Standard (+0)
- Effective roll: 2d6 + Knowledge(+1) + Standard(0) = 2d6+1

Vessa succeeded on both Inscription attempts (1 full, 1 failure on a different attempt targeting a warded lantern — a harder scenario).

**The gap is too large at pre-technique level.** Focused casters roll at +1 effective. Standard casters roll at +0 effective. Broad casters would roll at -1 effective (Hard → Very Hard → 2d6-1, which is nearly impossible). This means:

- Focused pre-technique magic: ~58% success rate at Minor — workable
- Standard pre-technique magic: ~42% success rate at Minor — frustrating
- Broad pre-technique magic: ~28% success rate at Minor — nearly non-functional

**Recommendation:** Consider one of:
- **(A)** Reduce pre-technique penalty from "one step harder" to "one step harder for Focused/Standard, two steps harder only for Broad." This preserves the Broad penalty while making Standard-type pre-technique magic viable.
- **(B)** Change the pre-technique penalty to only apply to Significant+ scope. Minor scope is already narratively "small, local, brief" — requiring it to be harder feels like punishing characters for having magic at all before their Technique.
- **(C)** Keep the penalty but give all magic-origin characters a free starting mark in Attune or Lore (whichever their tradition uses), similar to how Backgrounds give a secondary skill mark. This slightly offsets the penalty.

### 3. Spark Economy Was Under-Used

9 Sparks available (3 per character). Only 2 spent (both by Mira). The remaining 7 went unspent.

Reasons observed:
- **Hoarding instinct.** Players treated Sparks like 5e Inspiration or Fate Points — too precious to spend except in emergencies.
- **Unclear spending moments.** Players must spend Sparks *before* rolling. This means committing a scarce resource without knowing if it's necessary. Most rolls seemed "good enough" to attempt without spending.
- **No re-earn path in session.** Players earned 2 Sparks via post-session nomination, but none were earned mid-session (Jamie didn't award any, and no peer nominations happened during play). This means the supply felt fixed and diminishing.

**Recommendations:**
- **(A)** The MM Manual should explicitly remind MMs to award Sparks during play, not just at session end. "If no Spark has been earned by the session's midpoint, you're probably being too conservative." The system expects a flow of Sparks, not a static pool.
- **(B)** Consider a "Graceful Fail" reminder in the Quick Reference. When a player gets a 6- and leans into the consequence with great roleplay, the MM awards a Spark. This was described in the rules but never triggered during play because the MM forgot about it.
- **(C)** The digital tool should display a Spark reminder/prompt when a player rolls 6- (e.g., "Graceful Fail? Award a Spark if the player leaned into the consequence").

### 4. Mook Combat Is Fun but Mechanically Thin

The Mook fight was narratively satisfying but mechanically simple. Mooks don't react, have no Endurance, and fall to any successful Strike. The "tactical" part was posture choice and positioning, but since Mooks are Measured by default and always die in one hit, the optimal strategy is always Aggressive posture.

**The problem isn't Mooks themselves** — they're designed as "resource drain through action economy" and that works. The problem is that at Party Strength 3 with a Skirmish budget (TR 3), the fight is over before resource drain becomes relevant. The Mook fight was 2 exchanges. In a 2-exchange fight, Tier 1 conditions clear before they matter (they clear at end of exchange). Endurance costs from reactions were irrelevant because the fight ended before anyone ran low.

**Recommendation:** This may be a non-issue at higher party strengths and harder difficulties. At Standard (TR 6), a Mook swarm of 6 would create real action economy pressure. The Skirmish worked as intended — it was a quick intro to combat mechanics, not a resource drain. No system change needed, but MM2 should note: "Skirmish encounters are tutorial encounters. Don't expect tactical depth from them."

### 5. Group Stealth Roll Ruling Was Ad-Hoc

The MM improvised a "majority success" rule for group Stealth. The PHB doesn't specify how to handle group skill checks. This is a common TTRPG need (sneaking as a party, group navigation, collective investigation).

**Recommendation:** Add a sidebar to III.1 Core Resolution: "**Group Rolls:** When the whole party attempts the same task together, each character rolls. If the majority succeed (partial or better), the group succeeds. One standout success or failure may add narrative color. The MM may also designate a single 'lead' roller with others providing Support."

### Not Supported (Gaps)

| Action | Gap | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Technique tracking per character** | Character model stores `techniques: list[str]` but no UI to view/manage learned Techniques | Low impact — Techniques are tracked on paper/character sheet. Builder tab's technique selection handles the advancement, but there's no "active techniques" display during play. |
| **Condition auto-application from Strike outcomes** | After a Strike result, the MM must manually `apply_condition`. No automation of "10+ = Tier 2 condition, 7-9 = Tier 1" | Medium impact — adds MM workload. The tool could auto-suggest conditions based on Strike outcome tier. |
| **Endurance display for players** | Combat panel shows own Endurance but not other PCs' Endurance | Low impact — players can ask. Consider adding Endurance to the player list during combat. |
| **Armor downgrade automation** | `_downgrade_condition_for_armor()` exists in the backend but only applies when MM uses `apply_condition`. | **Spark spend on magic** | The `cast` handler accepts `spark_use` parameter but the magic panel UI doesn't integrate with the Spark pip counter from the roll panel | Low impact — player can manually set spark_use in the magic panel. The Spark pips in the roll panel don't auto-link. |
| **Background Specialty display** | Specialty is stored as free text on character but not displayed prominently | Low impact — could be shown in the character sheet or Tools tab. |

### Recommendations for Tool Development

1. **Auto-suggest conditions from Strike outcomes.** When a `strike_result` broadcasts, the MM controls could show a "Suggested: apply Tier 2 Staggered or Cornered to [target]" button, reducing manual overhead.

2. **Display all PC Endurance during combat.** Add Endurance bars to the player list when `state.inCombat` is true. Everyone should see the party's collective resource state.

3. **Add Technique display to character sheet.** Show learned Techniques (with descriptions) in both the Play tab character sheet and Tools tab. Techniques are core to the character's identity by mid-campaign.

4. **Graceful Fail Spark prompt.** When any roll produces a 6-, show a subtle prompt to the MM: "Graceful Fail — award a Spark?" This nudges the Spark economy toward its intended flow rate.

5. **NPC/enemy posture tracking.** Add a posture field to active enemies in the tracker. When the MM sets an enemy's posture, auto-adjust the difficulty label on reaction rolls against that enemy.

## SYSTEM BALANCE OBSERVATIONS

### Modifier Math

At starting level (career_advances 1), the typical modifier stack is:
- Attribute: +1 (if strong in that attribute) or +0/-1
- Skill: +1 (if Practiced) or +0
- Difficulty: +1 (Easy) to -2 (Very Hard)
- Posture: +1 (Aggressive) to -1 (Defensive)

**Effective modifier range at start:** -3 to +4

On 2d6, each +1 modifier shifts success rate by roughly 8-10 percentage points. The system's tight modifier range (compared to D&D's +3 to +11 at level 1) means every +1 matters noticeably. This is good — it makes attribute and skill choices feel impactful without creating dominant strategies.

### Endurance Economy

Kael (the tank) with Endurance 5 and light armor was the only character who could sustain more than 2 exchanges of active combat. Vessa and Mira (Endurance 4, no armor) would be forced to Absorb or Withdraw after 2 active reaction exchanges.

This creates a natural party dynamic: the Body character absorbs pressure while Mind/Soul characters contribute through lateral means (investigation, magic, support). It's working as designed.

**Concern:** At 0 Endurance, Absorb is the only option, and Absorbed conditions at 0 End are Tier 2. This creates a death spiral: once you're at 0 End, every hit is Tier 2, and two Tier 2 of the same type = Broken. A character at 0 End can be Broken in 2 hits with no agency.

**Recommendation:** The death spiral is intentional (combat should be dangerous) but may feel punitive for squishy characters. Monitor in future playtests. If characters regularly go Broken without meaningful choice, consider: "At 0 Endurance, you may spend a Spark to take a reaction at 0 cost one time."

### Spark Value

Each Spark adds a d6 and drops lowest. On 2d6, this shifts the average from ~7.0 to ~8.5 and pushes 10+ probability from ~17% to ~26%. On a Hard check (effective 2d6-1), spending a Spark brings success probability roughly back to Standard levels.

**Sparks are perfectly calibrated as "emergency normalization"** — they don't make you amazing, they prevent you from being terrible at a critical moment. The issue is purely behavioral (hoarding), not mechanical.

## PLAYER FEEDBACK SUMMARY

### Alex (Body — Kael)

The combat system is what I'd call 'tactically light but decision-heavy' — you make fewer choices than D&D but each one matters more. The Intercept costing 4 Endurance while Aggressive was the most stressful decision I've made in a game, and that's exactly what good design feels like.

> My concern is whether combat stays interesting at higher levels. Right now with 5 Endurance and one Technique slot (when I get there), fights are about resource management. Will it still feel tactical when I have 7-8 Endurance and multiple Techniques? Or will it just be 'I Strike, I Strike, I Strike' with occasional posture switches?

> Also: the double-sixes through the Defensive Ward re-roll was the best moment of the session. The system created that moment — the re-roll Technique added tension that wouldn't exist in a simpler system."

### Sam (Mind — Vessa)
> "The magic system is brilliant and terrifying. Brilliant because I can do literally anything within my domain's theme — I'm not picking from a spell list, I'm describing what I want. Terrifying because the MM has enormous authority over how that's interpreted. I said 'flash of disorienting light' and Jamie ruled it as a combat effect that kills a Mook. Another MM might have said 'it flinches, take +1 on your next roll.' The system needs good-faith collaboration between player and MM.

> Investigation was smooth. The Maneuver during the Guardian fight to find the lantern weakness was a mechanical action (roll Investigate during combat) that felt like a narrative action (examining the enemy). That's seamless integration.

> The pre-technique penalty was fine for me (Focused → Standard, effectively +1). I'm worried for Riley's Standard domain and anyone trying Broad. The math doesn't support casting Broad pre-technique at all."

### Riley (Soul — Mira)
> "I love the social system. Partial success on Insight gave me enough to be suspicious without solving the mystery. The MM didn't just say 'you think he's lying' — they gave me specific behavioral details I could act on. That's the PbtA DNA and it works.

> My magic was frustrating. Two failures out of three attempts. I know the math: Standard domain at pre-technique is Hard difficulty, which is basically a coin flip. The one success (the ward-lantern) only happened because I spent a Spark. Without Sparks, I would have gone 0 for 3.

> The Spark system needs the MM to actively award them mid-session. Jamie didn't award any during play — only the post-session nominations. If the earn rate was higher, I'd have spent more freely. It's a chicken-and-egg problem: players hoard because they don't expect to earn more, and MMs forget to award because players aren't spending.

> Best moment: the Intercept. Not my character — Kael's. But it happened because the system made it a real sacrifice (4 Endurance!) that Alex chose to make for a narrative reason. That's the kind of moment this system is built for."

### Jamie (Mirror Master)
> "Running this system was easier than D&D but harder than Fate. Easier because: no initiative tracking, no per-round action economy, no spell slot management, no complex NPC stat blocks. Harder because: every 7-9 requires me to invent a complication on the spot, and the magic system requires me to adjudicate intent/scope in real time.

> The encounter budget system worked perfectly. The Mook fight was a warmup (Skirmish), the Guardian fight was a real challenge (Standard). The TR calculation gave me confidence that the fights were calibrated correctly. The phase change was easy to run — just a narrative trigger that changed one mechanical parameter.

> My biggest frustration: the NPC combat rules have gaps. How does an NPC's posture affect PC reactions? What tier is Absorb against different enemy types? I made three rulings on the fly that felt reasonable but aren't documented. For a system that prides itself on simplicity, these ambiguities create friction.

> I would run this system again. The session-to-session progression (4 skill points, marking used skills) is simple enough that I wouldn't dread advancement bookkeeping. The character creation speed means losing a character to Broken isn't devastating — you could make a new one in 15 minutes."

## PRIORITIZED RECOMMENDATIONS

### Must Fix (Before Next Playtest)

1. **Clarify NPC attack rules in III.3 Combat.** Specifically: how NPC posture modifiers affect reaction difficulty, what tier Absorb receives from different enemy types (Mook/Named/Boss), and whether armor and reaction downgrades stack.

2. **Add Group Roll rules to III.1 Core Resolution.** A sidebar covering "majority success" for when the whole party attempts a task together.

### Should Fix (Before v0.2 Release)

3. **Re-evaluate pre-technique magic penalty for Standard/Broad domains.** The current penalty makes Standard pre-technique magic a coin flip and Broad pre-technique magic nearly impossible. Consider applying the penalty only to Significant+ scope, or reducing it for non-Focused domains.

4. **Add Spark economy guidance to MM5 Quick Reference.** A reminder: "Award at least 1-2 Sparks mid-session. Use Graceful Fail. If no Spark has been earned by the midpoint, you're being too conservative."

5. **Add auto-suggest conditions and Endurance display to the digital tool.** Reduces MM cognitive load during combat.

### Nice to Have (Future)

6. **Technique display in the digital tool.** Both during play (Play tab) and for reference (Tools tab).

7. **NPC/enemy posture tracking in the enemy tracker.** With auto-difficulty adjustment for reactions.

8. **Specialty mechanical integration.** The digital tool should show the character's Specialty prominently and could auto-flag when a roll falls within the Specialty's domain.

## CONCLUSION

Facets of Origin v0.1 delivered on its core promise: a fast, narrative-first TTRPG where the rules never got in the way of the story. Character creation took 15 minutes. Combat took 10-20 minutes per encounter and produced genuinely tactical decisions. Magic was creative and flexible. Social encounters were mechanically supported without being mechanically dominated.

The system's weaknesses are mostly in documentation (ambiguous NPC combat rules, missing group roll guidance) rather than design. The one genuine design concern — pre-technique magic scaling — is a tuning issue, not a structural one.

The digital tool supports ~90% of the playtest's mechanical actions natively. The gaps (Technique re-rolls, auto-condition suggestion, group rolls) are addressable with targeted feature additions. The three-tab web app (Play/Tools/Builder) maps well to the session's flow: Play for rolling and combat, Tools for reference and character sheets, Builder for advancement.

**Verdict: Ready for real-table playtesting with the three "Must Fix" documentation clarifications.**
