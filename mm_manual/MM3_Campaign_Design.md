# Mirror Master's Manual: Campaign Design

## Overview

A single session of Facets of Origin is a story. A campaign is a story made of stories — sessions that accumulate into something larger than any one of them could have been alone. The characters grow. The world responds. The things that happened three sessions ago turn out to matter in ways nobody planned.

This chapter is about designing and running that longer arc. Not in the sense of writing a novel and dragging players through it, but in the sense of building structures that generate story naturally — frameworks that give sessions momentum, characters a reason to keep showing up, and you, the Mirror Master, enough material to run on without drowning in preparation.

The advice here assumes you have already run a session or two. If you have not, start with a one-shot. Get comfortable with the exchange structure, the 2d6 resolution system, and the feeling of narrating outcomes before you commit to a longer arc. A campaign is not harder than a one-shot — it is just longer, and "longer" compounds both your good instincts and your bad ones.

---

## Campaign Structures

Every campaign has a shape, whether you choose one deliberately or discover it three sessions in when you realize you have been running a sandbox the whole time. Knowing the shapes helps you pick the right one — or recognize what you have already built and lean into it.

### Episodic

Each session is a self-contained story. The party solves a problem, defeats a threat, or uncovers a mystery, and the next session begins with a new situation. Characters persist; the world persists; but the plotlines do not carry mandatory homework from one week to the next.

**Best for:** Groups with irregular attendance, new players, tables that want variety, campaigns where players might rotate in and out. Also excellent when you are still learning what your group enjoys — each session is a fresh experiment.

**Risks:** It can feel like nothing matters beyond the current session. Characters develop mechanically but not narratively unless you deliberately thread connections between episodes. The solution is recurring NPCs, a shared home base, and consequences that echo forward even when the plot does not.

**Pacing:** Each session should have a clear beginning, middle, and resolution. Plan your sessions to be completable in one sitting. If an episode runs long, end it at the next natural pause — do not carry half-finished episodic plots into the next week.

---

### Arc-Based

The campaign is built from arcs of 3–5 sessions, each telling a complete story within a larger narrative. An arc has its own antagonist, its own stakes, and its own resolution. Between arcs, the fiction breathes — the party returns to base, relationships develop, and the seeds for the next arc are planted.

**Best for:** Most groups. Arc-based campaigns offer the satisfaction of long-term narrative without requiring perfect attendance or total recall of plot threads from eight sessions ago. Each arc is a promise: this story will resolve. Players trust you because you keep that promise.

**Risks:** The transition between arcs is where campaigns stall. The climax of Arc 1 was exciting; the setup for Arc 2 feels like homework. Solve this by ending each arc with a question, not an answer. The villain is defeated — but who sent them? The artifact is recovered — but what does the inscription mean? The question pulls the table forward into the next arc without requiring you to have planned Arc 2 before Arc 1 ended.

**Pacing:** Sessions 1–2 of an arc are setup and investigation. Session 3 is escalation — the stakes become personal. Sessions 4–5 are the climax and aftermath. Not every arc needs all five sessions; some of the best arcs are three sessions of mounting tension and a single explosive resolution.

---

### Sandbox

The world exists. The players choose where to go and what to do. Your job is to populate the world with interesting things and react to what the players poke. There is no predetermined plot — there are factions with agendas, locations with secrets, and consequences that follow from player decisions.

**Best for:** Experienced groups who enjoy player agency more than narrative structure. Tables where the players are proactive and the MM is comfortable improvising. Groups who want to feel like they are exploring a world, not following a story.

**Risks:** Sandboxes demand more preparation of the wrong kind and less of the right kind. You do not need a plot — you need a world that generates plots when the players interact with it. That means factions with goals that advance between sessions, locations with things happening in them whether or not the players show up, and NPCs who want things and will pursue those wants. The risk is not that nothing happens; it is that the players do not know where to start. Give them three obvious hooks in session one and let them choose.

**Pacing:** Sandboxes have no inherent pacing. You impose it by having the world move. If the players spend three sessions shopping and socializing, that is fine — but the cult in the mountains did not stop their ritual because the party was buying new boots. World events create urgency when the players need it.

---

### Mystery-Box

The campaign is organized around a central question that the party gradually uncovers the answer to. Who destroyed the old kingdom? What is behind the barrier? Why do people keep disappearing? Every session peels back a layer, and the answer to the central question recontextualizes everything the party has experienced.

**Best for:** Tables that love investigation, theorizing between sessions, and the slow burn of revelation. Groups where the players enjoy talking about the game when they are not playing it. MMs who are comfortable holding a secret for a long time and letting the players construct their own theories.

**Risks:** You must actually have an answer. The single greatest failure mode of a mystery-box campaign is a MM who poses a fascinating question and has no idea what the answer is. You do not need every detail — but you need the shape of the answer from session one, because the clues you plant must be consistent in retrospect. The second risk is that the players figure it out early. This is not a failure. When players solve your mystery in session four of eight, the remaining sessions become about what they do with the knowledge. That is often more interesting than the reveal would have been.

**Pacing:** Clue distribution is the heartbeat. Every session should offer at least one piece of the puzzle. Not every piece needs to be central — some are red herrings, some are context, some are emotional rather than informational. But a session with zero new information about the central question will leave mystery-box players frustrated.

---

### Hybrid Structures

Most real campaigns are hybrids. An arc-based campaign with a mystery-box question running underneath it. An episodic campaign that gradually reveals a sandbox world. A sandbox with a ticking clock that converts it into an arc when the players finally notice. Do not feel obligated to pick one structure and commit. Use the structure that serves your table this week.

## The Campaign Pitch

Before you build anything, write one paragraph. One. It should tell the players what this campaign is about — the tone, the premise, the kind of story, and what their characters will be doing. If you cannot write that paragraph, you are not ready to run session one. If the paragraph is longer than one paragraph, you are overbuilding.

The campaign pitch is a contract. It tells the players what to expect so they can build characters that belong in this story. A player who builds a courtly diplomat for a dungeon-crawl pitch did not fail — you failed to communicate.

### Example Pitches

**Episodic — "The Thornwall Watch"**

> You are members of the Thornwall City Watch — not the elite investigators, not the governor's guard, but the district officers who handle the things nobody else wants to deal with. Every session is a new case: a missing merchant, a haunted tenement, a dockside brawl that turns out to be something worse. The tone is equal parts police procedural and fantasy comedy. Build characters who have a reason to show up for work every day, even when the work is terrible.

**Arc-Based — "The Shattered Road"**

> A trade route between two cities has collapsed — not physically, but socially. Caravans are turning back. Travelers are vanishing. The settlements along the road have gone quiet. You have been hired to find out why, reopen the route, and deal with whatever is responsible. The campaign will run in arcs: each settlement along the road is its own story, and the cause behind the collapse will become clear as you travel deeper. Build characters who have a reason to be on the road and a reason to care about the people along it.

**Sandbox — "The Free Marches"**

> The southern frontier is ungoverned. The old empire withdrew thirty years ago and left behind ruins, feuding towns, and a lot of unanswered questions about what was happening out here before they pulled out. You are here — for your own reasons — and what you do with this place is up to you. There are factions, opportunities, and threats. None of them are waiting for you. Build characters who want something they can only find out here.

**Mystery-Box — "The Silence"**

> Six months ago, the Oracle of Greymount stopped speaking. She had spoken continuously for three hundred years — prophecy, warning, guidance — and then, mid-sentence, she stopped. She is alive. Her eyes are open. She does not respond. You have been sent to find out what happened. The answer will take you further than you expect. Build characters who are drawn to questions they cannot leave unanswered.

## NPC Design

### People, Not Stat Blocks

The NPCs your players remember will not be the ones with the most interesting stat blocks. They will be the ones who felt like people — who wanted something, who surprised the table, who showed up again when nobody expected them.

When you build a Named NPC who will appear in more than one session, give them three things:

1. **One want.** Something they are actively pursuing. Not a vague motivation — a specific, immediate goal. The archivist wants to retire before the new governor takes office. The blacksmith wants her daughter to leave town before the harvest festival. The merchant wants to buy a specific piece of land and will not explain why.

2. **One secret.** Something they know or have done that they are not sharing. The secret does not need to be dramatic — it just needs to be true in a way that would change how the party sees them. The cheerful innkeeper is in debt to a local crime lord. The priest has not heard their god's voice in three years and is terrified.

3. **One connection to a PC.** This is the hook that makes the NPC matter to the table, not just the world. The connection can be established at creation ("she's the cousin Mordai mentioned in his background") or discovered in play ("you recognize the pendant — it's from your hometown"). NPCs without PC connections are scenery. NPCs with them are story.

### Making NPCs Memorable in Thirty Seconds

You will introduce dozens of NPCs across a campaign. Most of them do not need three-dimensional backstories. They need to be immediately distinct so the players can remember them.

The trick is **one specific detail** — not a personality summary, but a concrete, sensory thing the players can latch onto.

- "The guard has an accent you cannot place and keeps cracking her knuckles."
- "He introduces himself while eating an apple and does not stop eating the apple for the entire conversation."
- "She speaks very quietly, and every time she does, the room gets quieter too."

One detail is enough. Players will fill in the rest. If you try to describe an NPC's entire personality upfront, the players will remember none of it. If you give them one vivid image, they will remember it for the whole campaign.

### Recurring vs. One-Shot NPCs

**One-shot NPCs** serve the current session. They deliver information, create obstacles, or provide color. They need a name and one detail. Do not over-invest in them — some will become recurring NPCs organically, and that is fine. The shopkeeper you improvised in session two who made the table laugh will come back. The one you spent an hour designing will not. Accept this.

**Recurring NPCs** are characters in the campaign's story. They grow, they change, they react to what the party does. After each session, ask yourself: *what does this NPC think about what just happened?* If you cannot answer that question, the NPC is not recurring — they are scenery with a name.

The best recurring NPCs are the ones who start as one-shot NPCs and get promoted. When the table latches onto someone, give that NPC a want, a secret, and a connection. Now they are part of the story.

## World-Building at the Right Scale

### Start With the Town, Not the Cosmology

New MMs build top-down: the creation myth, the pantheon, the geopolitical structure of three continents. This is enjoyable. It is also almost entirely useless in play. Your players will never ask about the trade relationships between two kingdoms they have not visited. They will ask what the tavern is called, whether the blacksmith can fix Mordai's sword, and why that old woman keeps staring at them from across the square.

Build outward from what the players can see. Session one needs:

- **A location.** The town, the ship, the camp — wherever the story starts. It needs a name, two or three landmarks the players can reference, and one thing that is wrong or unusual about it. That is your starting hook.
- **Three NPCs.** One who gives them information, one who wants something from them, and one who is watching. You can combine these if you are feeling efficient.
- **One problem.** The thing that makes session one a story instead of a tour.

Everything else — the wider world, the political landscape, the ancient history — can be built as the players encounter it. And when you build it at the table, it will be better than what you would have built alone, because it will be shaped by what the players have already done and cared about.

### The Concentric Circles Approach

Think of your world as concentric circles radiating outward from the party's current position:

**Inner circle (fully detailed):** The immediate environment — the town, the dungeon, the ship. You know the NPCs by name, you know the layout, you know what is happening here right now. This is where you prep.

**Middle circle (sketched):** The surrounding region — neighboring towns, the nearest city, the road network, major factions operating in the area. You have names, a sentence of description for each, and a rough sense of what is happening. You can improvise details when players ask.

**Outer circle (implied):** The wider world — other nations, distant histories, the cosmology. You know the broad strokes. You have names for things and a general sense of tone. If a player asks a specific question about the outer circle, you make something up and write it down. It is canon now.

Each session, the inner circle moves with the party. What was the middle circle last session becomes the inner circle when the players travel there — and you detail it then, not before.

### When to Prep vs. When to Improvise

**Prep** when: the players have announced they are going somewhere specific next session; you are introducing a major NPC; you are running a set-piece encounter; the session involves a mystery with clues that need to be consistent.

**Improvise** when: the players go somewhere you did not expect; they ask about an NPC you did not plan; they ask a world-building question you have not considered; they solve a problem in a way that makes your prepared material irrelevant.

The ratio should be roughly 60/40 improvisation to prep in a healthy campaign. If you are prepping more than you are improvising, you are either over-preparing or your players are unusually predictable. If you are improvising more than 80%, you are likely losing consistency — write things down after you improvise them so they stay true next session.

**The cardinal rule of improvisation:** Write it down. The name you invent at the table is now the name. The detail you add to explain something the players noticed is now the truth. Your future self will thank your present self for the three words you scribbled in the margin.

## Encounter Sequencing

### Difficulty Should Breathe

A campaign where every encounter is Hard is exhausting. A campaign where every encounter is a Skirmish is boring. Difficulty should rise and fall across sessions like a breathing pattern — tension, release, tension, release, with the peaks getting higher as the arc progresses.

A useful default for a 4-session arc:

| Session | Encounter Difficulty | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Skirmish or none | Establish the situation; let players investigate and plan |
| 2 | Standard | First real resistance; the stakes become clear |
| 3 | Hard or Standard | Escalation; a setback or complication forces adaptation |
| 4 | Hard or Deadly | Climax; the arc resolves |

This is a guideline, not a formula. Some arcs work better with the hardest fight in session two and the climax as a social confrontation. Some arcs have no combat at all. The point is that difficulty should escalate toward the moments that matter most in the story.

### The Resource Tax

Every encounter — combat or otherwise — costs the party something. Endurance, Sparks, information leverage, NPC goodwill, time. The resource tax is the cumulative cost of everything the party has spent getting to this point.

In a single session, the resource tax is straightforward: the party enters the climactic encounter with whatever Endurance and Sparks they have left after the earlier encounters. This is why a Skirmish before a Hard encounter is useful — it is not about the Skirmish itself, it is about the 2–3 Endurance points the party spent getting through it.

Across multiple sessions, the resource tax is more subtle. It is the NPC the party alienated in session two who will not help them in session four. It is the information they chose not to pursue that would have made the final encounter easier. It is the Spark they spent on a low-stakes moment that they will wish they had saved.

Track what the party has spent — not in a ledger, but in your awareness of the fiction. When they arrive at the climax, the story should reflect what they invested to get there.

### Not Every Session Needs a Fight

This should be obvious, but it is easy to forget when you are staring at a blank session plan: **combat is a tool, not an obligation.** Some of the most memorable sessions in any campaign are the ones where the dice barely come out — the negotiation with the antagonist, the quiet evening in town between arcs, the session where the party finally talks to each other about what happened.

If you plan a fight for every session, your players will learn that every session has a fight, and they will stop being surprised by combat. If you occasionally run a session with no combat at all, the session where the fight does happen will hit harder.

A rough guideline: in a 4-session arc, 2–3 sessions should involve combat. At least one session should be primarily investigation, social interaction, or exploration. The exact mix depends on your table — some groups want more fights, some want fewer. Ask them.

## Campaign Arcs and Advancement

### Advancement as Story Structure

The Facets advancement system is not just a reward mechanism — it is a pacing tool. Each mechanical threshold corresponds to a natural story beat, and you can use those thresholds to structure your campaign.

| Threshold | Approximate Timeline | Story Beat |
|---|---|---|
| First skill rank advance | Sessions 1–3 | The character finds their footing; early competence emerges |
| Facet Level 1 (6 primary advances) | Sessions 8–15 | The character has a clear identity; first Technique defines their approach |
| Facet Level 2 (12 primary advances) | Sessions 20–30 | The character is established; cross-training begins; breadth over depth |
| Major Advancement (4 Facet levels total) | Sessions 35–50+ | The character has become someone — the +1 Attribute or Pinnacle Technique marks a transformation |

These timelines assume 4 skill points per session and primary Facet advancement. Actual pace depends on how the player distributes their points, whether they cross-train early, and how often they use the skills they want to advance.

### Using career_advances as a Campaign Clock

Every character's `career_advances` is a single number that tells you how far they have come. It is also a useful planning tool.

When you are designing an arc, ask: *what career_advances range is this party in?* The answer tells you what kind of challenges are appropriate, what kinds of enemies will feel threatening, and how much the party can handle in a single session.

| career_advances | Party Capability | Encounter Design Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 0–2 | Fragile, narrow | Mooks are genuine threats in groups; one Named NPC is a boss fight |
| 3–6 | Developing, gaining tools | Standard encounters feel fair; introduce Named NPC antagonists |
| 7–12 | Capable, first Techniques | Hard encounters are survivable; Mook swarms are cleanup; Bosses are viable |
| 13–20 | Seasoned, cross-trained | The party has answers to most problems; challenge them with situations, not raw difficulty |
| 21+ | Veterans | Difficulty alone will not challenge them; the stakes must be narrative, not mechanical |

### Pacing Advancement Faster or Slower

The default is 4 skill points per session, with primary Facet skills costing 1 point per mark and cross-Facet skills costing 2. This produces a steady, moderate pace of advancement. But you can adjust this to serve your campaign.

**Faster advancement** — for short campaigns (8–12 sessions) where you want the party to reach Facet Level 1 and feel the Technique unlock:
- Award 5–6 skill points per session instead of 4
- Allow an extra mark for skills used in particularly dramatic moments
- Run shorter arcs so the advancement feels dense

**Slower advancement** — for long campaigns (30+ sessions) where you want growth to feel earned and the early phases to breathe:
- Keep the default 4 skill points
- Let cross-Facet training cost what it costs — the breadth will come when the story supports it
- Use the longer timeline between Facet levels to develop the character's narrative arc before the mechanical transformation arrives

**Never** withhold advancement as punishment. Skill points are earned by using skills, not by pleasing the MM. If a player used a skill this session, they can spend points on it. The advancement system is a reward for engagement, and engagement should always be rewarded.

### Techniques as Narrative Milestones

When a character reaches a new Facet level and unlocks a Technique, that is a story event — not just a mechanical upgrade. The reflection scene is your tool for making it feel real.

Plan for it. When you see a character approaching their next Facet level (check their marks — you can see it coming 2–3 sessions out), start thinking about what moment in the fiction could serve as the backdrop for their advancement. The warrior who unlocks Weapon Mastery after a fight where they finally understood how their sword works. The scholar who unlocks Analytical Eye after a session where every detail they noticed turned out to matter. The diplomat who unlocks Commanding Presence after the scene where they stood in front of a hostile crowd and did not flinch.

The Technique is the mechanic. The reflection scene is the story. Together, they make the advancement feel like something that happened to a person, not a number that incremented.

## Ending a Campaign

### Recognizing When It Is Done

Campaigns die in two ways: they end, or they trail off. One of these is satisfying. The other leaves everyone vaguely disappointed and slightly guilty about not scheduling another session.

The signs that a campaign is reaching its natural end:

- **The central question has been answered.** The mystery is solved, the villain is defeated, the journey is complete. There may be loose threads, but the main arc has resolved.
- **The characters have arrived.** They have become the people the campaign was about them becoming. Their mechanical advancement reflects a complete arc — first Facet Level, maybe a Major Advancement, cross-training that shows how the journey changed them.
- **The table's energy has shifted.** Players are talking about what they want to play next. Session scheduling gets harder. The between-session conversations have moved from speculation about the campaign to excitement about other things. This is not failure — it is the natural rhythm of collaborative storytelling. Stories end.

When you see these signs, talk to the table. "I think we are approaching the end of this story. Does everyone feel that?" If the answer is yes, plan the ending. If the answer is "no, we want to keep going," listen to why — they may see a story thread you have missed.

### Planning the Final Session

The final session of a campaign should feel like a final session. It should be special — not in the sense of bigger fights or more dramatic stakes, but in the sense of completeness. The characters should encounter the thing the whole campaign has been building toward, and the outcome should feel earned.

Structure the final session around three beats:

1. **The approach.** The party prepares for the final confrontation — whatever form it takes. This is where the resources they have gathered, the allies they have earned, and the knowledge they have accumulated all come together. Let them plan. Let them feel how far they have come.

2. **The climax.** The central conflict resolves. This might be a fight, a negotiation, a ritual, a revelation — whatever the campaign demands. It should require the party to use everything they have learned. The outcome should reflect their choices across the entire campaign, not just this session.

3. **The aftermath.** The dust settles. The world is different. What does that look like? This is where you give the players time to breathe and to see the consequences of what they have done.

### Character Epilogues

After the final session, offer each player a moment to describe what their character does next. Not in detail — a sentence or two. Where do they go? What do they pursue? How did this story change them?

Epilogues are optional. Some players want them; some prefer to leave the ending open. Ask, do not impose. But when a player takes their epilogue, give them the full attention of the table. This character existed for weeks or months of play. Their ending deserves to be heard.

### Not Dragging It Out

The most common campaign failure is not ending badly — it is not ending at all. The story reaches its natural conclusion around session fifteen, but nobody says so, and the campaign limps along for another eight sessions of declining energy and increasing guilt.

If the story is done, end it. A campaign that ends at its peak is remembered fondly. A campaign that overstays its welcome is remembered as "that game we should have wrapped up months ago." Be brave enough to say "I think this is the end," and trust that the next campaign will be even better because you ended this one well.

A ten-session campaign that tells a complete, satisfying story is better than a forty-session campaign where the last fifteen were obligation. Every time.

## In Play: The End of the Road

*The party has spent four arcs — sixteen sessions — traveling the Shattered Road, reopening trade between Thornwall and the coastal settlements. They have fought bandits, negotiated with feuding mayors, uncovered a conspiracy to keep the road closed, and finally confronted the merchant house responsible. The final confrontation has ended — not in a fight, but in a tense negotiation where Zahna presented the evidence, Mordai stood behind him looking like a very large visual aid, and Zulnut quietly pocketed the merchant lord's seal ring during the handshake. The road is open. The campaign is ending.*

---

**MM:** "Three weeks later. The first full caravan in two years rolls into Thornwall. The governor has organized a reception — bunting, speeches, a feast in the market square. The three of you are invited to stand on the platform. Zahna, you have been asked to give a short address."

**Zahna:** "Define 'short.'"

**MM:** "Fewer than four hundred words. The governor was very specific."

**Zahna:** "I have prepared remarks."

(The MM closes their eyes briefly.)

**Mordai:** "Are they good?"

**Zahna:** "They are accurate."

**Mordai:** "That is not the same thing."

*Zahna delivers a precise, thorough, and deeply boring summary of the trade route's historical significance, the administrative failures that led to its closure, and a seventeen-point recommendation for infrastructure investment. The crowd is polite. The governor's smile does not waver, which is its own kind of achievement.*

**MM:** "Mordai, you are standing behind him on the platform. What are you doing?"

**Mordai:** "Scanning the crowd. Looking for threats."

**MM:** "There are no threats. It is a party."

**Mordai:** "There are always threats."

(There are no threats. He looks anyway. The MM decides this is the most Mordai thing that has ever happened.)

**MM:** "Zulnut. You are notably absent from the platform."

**Zulnut:** "I'm at the feast table. I've been at the feast table since before the speeches started."

**MM:** "Of course you have."

*Zulnut is eating something with both hands, leaning against a pillar, watching the crowd from a position that happens to have clear sightlines to every exit. He notices the pickpocket working the edge of the crowd — a kid, maybe twelve, very good hands — and does nothing about it except nod approvingly.*

**MM:** "The speeches end. The feast begins properly. The sun is setting over Thornwall, and the road — the one you spent four months reopening — stretches south toward the coast, and for the first time in two years, there are lights moving along it."

*A pause.*

**MM:** "What do you do next?"

---

**Zahna:** "There are records in the coastal archive that referenced a second collapse — older, from before the empire. I want to know what caused it."

**Mordai:** "Someone has to walk that road and make sure it stays open. I will do that."

**Zulnut:** "I'm going to finish eating. Then I'm going to take a nap." *A beat.* "Then probably south. Mordai will need someone watching his back, and I have nothing better to do."

(The MM smiles. The campaign is over. The story is not — it never is — but this part of it is complete. The characters are different than when they started. The world is different because they were in it. That is enough. That is always enough.)

## Quick Reference: Campaign Planning Checklist

```
Before Session 1:
  □ Campaign pitch — one paragraph, shared with players before character creation
  □ Campaign structure chosen (or intentionally hybrid)
  □ Starting location detailed (name, 2–3 landmarks, one problem)
  □ 3 starting NPCs (want, secret, PC connection — at least for the key one)
  □ Session 1 hook — the thing that makes this a story, not a tour

Before Each Arc:
  □ Arc question — what is this arc about? One sentence.
  □ Arc antagonist or obstacle — who or what stands in the way?
  □ Encounter difficulty plan — rough sense of the ebb and flow
  □ NPC check — who is relevant? Who has changed since last arc?

Before Each Session:
  □ One prepared scene — the thing you know will happen
  □ Two flexible hooks — things that could happen depending on player choices
  □ NPC status — what do your recurring NPCs want right now?
  □ Advancement check — is anyone approaching a Facet level or Major Advancement?

After Each Session:
  □ Write down everything you improvised — names, places, facts, promises
  □ Update NPC reactions — what do they think about what just happened?
  □ Note which player characters advanced and what skills they used
  □ Check: is the arc approaching its climax, or does it need another session?
  □ Check: is the campaign approaching its end?
```
